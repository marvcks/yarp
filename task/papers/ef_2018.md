ABSTRACT: Biomass pyrolysis is a promising technology for the production of bio-oil and chemicals. Among these pyrolytic products, furans are important chemicals. In this study, PY-GC×GC-TOFMS was employed to achieve fast pyrolysis of $\beta$ -D-glucose and online analysis of the pyrolysis vapors. The effects of pyrolysis temperature on the distribution of main furans are investigated. 3-Hydroxy- $\gamma$ -butyrolactone (HBL) and 3-(2H)-furanone (3FO) are observed to be the major $C_4$ furans which are involved in the evolution of $C_4$ intermediates in fast pyrolysis. Thus, density functional theory (DFT) calculations and pyrolysis of $^{13}C$ -labeled glucose were combined to reveal possible formation pathways from different intermediates, carbon sources of HBL and 3FO. The results indicated that the production of HBL and that of 3FO are from similar pathways and show a competitive manner in the pyrolysis of glucose. Moreover, HBL and 3FO are confirmed to be mainly derived from C3-C6 of glucose, the possible pathways for the formation of the two furans are also proposed. Mechanism studies here can assist the understanding for the key evolution of $C_4$ intermediates and contribute to support catalyst design in biomass pyrolysis.

# 1. INTRODUCTION

Thermochemical conversion of biomass to bio-oil and high value chemicals is an important development direction of biomass utilization. $^{1-3}$ Among the biomass pyrolytic products, the furan product is one of the most important chemicals. Cellulose is the most abundant component of biomass and is an attractive feedstock to produce furans. As a monomer of cellulose, $\beta$ -D-glucose is an important intermediate in cellulose pyrolysis, $^{4,5}$ the higher yields of furans from glucose than that from cellulose and cellulose suggested that low-polymerized sugars are more easy for pyrolysis to furans. $^{6,7}$ Thus, the understanding of $\beta$ -D-glucose pyrolysis mechanism is required to comprehend the cellulose and biomass thermal conversion process.

As to furans, furfural (FF) and 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) are considered to be a promising platform molecule used to produce various valuable chemicals. [8,9] And, the formation of FF and HMF from pyrolysis of cellulose has been studied for decades. [10-14] However, much less attention has been given to the formation mechanism of other furans compared with FF and HMF because of their lower yields. Nonetheless, 3-(2H)-furanone (3FO) and 3-hydroxy- $\gamma$ -butyrolactone (HBL) are the direct products of $\mathrm{C_4}$ fragments, which relate to some $\mathrm{C_2}$ product pathways and the most abundant $\mathrm{C_4}$ furans. Thus, the two furans directly represent the formation of the main $\mathrm{C_4}$ furans in furan products and one of the reaction routes of $\mathrm{C_4}$ intermediates during the pyrolysis of glucose. Moreover, HBL and 3FO are also important synthetic intermediates or molecular skeletons for a variety of chiral compounds. [15,16] Mechanism studies on these furans can help to clarify the key reactions involved in the formation of $\mathrm{C_4}$

furans and contribute to the development of selective pyrolysis techniques.

Density functional theory $^{17,18}$ (DFT) has been extensively used to study the chemical reactions during cellulose pyrolysis. $^{19-21}$ Zhang et al. $^{19}$ found that the $\mathrm{C_4}$ fragment was formed via retro-Diels-Alder reaction of $\beta$ D-glucose. Lu et al. $^{20}$ investigated the formation pathways of glycolaldehyde (HAA) and the possible formation mechanism of $\mathrm{C_4}$ fragments via 1,3-dehydration and retro-Diels-Alder. Zhang et al. $^{21}$ studied the mechanism of four kinds of pyran ring breaking, they found that retro-Diels-Alder and retro-aldol are the two kinetic favored pathways. Recently, a $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ isotope labeling technique was employed to investigate the formation mechanism of furans during glucose pyrolysis and proposed many formation mechanism of furans including HMF, FF, and 3FO. $^{22}$ However, the evolution of $\mathrm{C_4}$ intermediates and the formation paths of HBL and 3FO are still uncertain. Moreover, in building a mechanistic model of glucose, several studies have proposed and improved their kinetic models using the elementary reaction mechanisms from experimental and computational studies. $^{23-26}$ These models provide information that is inaccessible directly from experiments and help us to understand the pyrolysis process. However, due to the complexity of the glucose pyrolysis, additional chemical reactions and mechanisms are needed to improve and expand the current mechanistic model. $^{25}$ Hence, it is necessary for the approach of the correct mechanism of glucose cracking. # 2. METHODS

2.1. Samples. The $\beta$ -D-glucose sample was purchased from the TCI Company, the purity of $\beta$ -D-glucose is over $85\%$ with $\alpha$ -D-glucose ( $\sim 15\%$ ). It should be noted that the conformation of $\beta$ -D-glucose is more stable than $\alpha$ -D-glucose. In aqueous solution, the anomers come to equilibrium with just over one-third of the population of glucose molecules in the $\alpha$ conformation.[27] Under the pyrolysis conditions, $\alpha$ -D-glucose and $\beta$ -D-glucose can also be interconverted.

However, during the pyrolysis process, both forms of glucose will first undergo ring-opening reaction with similar energy barriers[28] to convert into ring-opened glucose in the form 3FO and HBL. Furthermore, in cellulose pyrolysis, the yields of HBL are also similar to glucose.[6] Hence, it was believed that the presence of $\alpha$ -D-glucose has little effect on the formation mechanism of HBL and 3FO from $\beta$ -D-glucose.

2.2. Pyrolysis Experiment. The typical pyrolysis of glucose and pyrolysis of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled glucose were performed in different devices. The details of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled Py-GC/MS experiments can be found in the Supporting Information. The typical pyrolysis of glucose samples was performed in a fast pyrolyzer (CDS pyroprobe 5200). A $0.3\mathrm{mg}$ glucose sample was loaded in the quartz tube with quartz wool like a sandwich, and glucose samples were located in the middle with quartz wool outside. The final pyrolysis temperature was set at $350 - 550^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , the heating rate was $20000^{\circ}\mathrm{C} / \mathrm{s}$ , and the residence time was $10\mathrm{s}$ . We need to point out that the residence time in this study means char residence time but not volatile; actually for volatiles, it is less than a second. The pyrolysis vapor was swept to a two-dimensional gas chromatography-time of flight mass spectrometer (GC×GC-TOF/MS) promptly for species analysis. The GC×GC is Dani Master serials, and it is equipped with an HP-5 column (length $30\mathrm{m}$ , internal diameter $250\mu \mathrm{m}$ , film thickness $0.25\mu \mathrm{m}$ ) and DB-1 (length $1\mathrm{m}$ , internal diameter $100\mu \mathrm{m}$ , film thickness $0.1\mu \mathrm{m}$ ) column. The mass detector was a DANI time-of-flight mass detector. The modem was ZOEX-1 (USA) equipped with a liquid nitrogen supply system. The injector temperature was kept at $280^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . During vapors analysis, the oven temperature was initially maintained at $40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for $5\mathrm{min}$ , then increased to $280^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ at a heating rate of $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C} / \mathrm{min}$ . The modulation period was kept at $5\mathrm{s}$ , and liquid nitrogen height was kept at $30 - 40\%$ . The total nitrogen pressure was $50~\mathrm{psi}$ and cold nitrogen flow rate was $20~\mathrm{L} / \mathrm{min}$ . The mass spectrometer was operated in electron ionization (EI) mode at $70\mathrm{eV}$ . The mass spectra were obtained from $m/z$ 40 to 500 with the scan rate of $500\mathrm{Da} / \mathrm{s}$ . Helium $(99.999\%)$ was used as the carrier gas with a constant flow rate of $1\mathrm{mL} / \mathrm{min}$ and a split ratio of 1:200. The chromatographic peak was identified according to the NIST MS library and literature data of previous studies.[22,29]

Each sample was tested for at least three trials to confirm the repeatability. The average values of peak area $\%$ were used for analysis. It is known that the MS technique could not give the quantitative analysis of each compound. However, the peak volume of a compound is considered linear with its quantity, and the peak area $\%$ is linear with its content. Therefore, the change of peak area $\%$ revealed the selectivity change, and the change of peak area revealed the yield change.

2.3. Computational Methods. The density functional theory (DFT) method based on quantum mechanics is used here. The geometries of reactants, intermediates, transition states, and products were optimized using the Gaussian 09 suite of programs. $^{30}$ The M06- $2\mathrm{X}^{31}$ functional with $6 - 311 + + \mathrm{g}(\mathrm{d},\mathrm{p})$ basis set was used as it has been

shown to accurately estimate energy barriers and successfully employed in the studies of carbohydrates conversion. $^{12,28,32,33}$ To obtain the standard thermodynamic parameters and ensure the transition states have sole imaginary frequency while others have no imaginary frequency, frequency analysis was also conducted with the same method. Intrinsic reaction coordinate (IRC) analysis was performed to verify the transition states correlating to the correct reactants and products. Activation energies of the reactions are estimated as the energy differences between the transition states and the reactants, including zero-point energy correction (ZPE). Free energies are used for discussions on energetics, under the standard condition $(298.15\mathrm{K}$ and 1 atm). The reaction rate constants for rate-determining steps were computed at $773\mathrm{K}$ using TST with Wigner tunneling correction, as implemented in the KiSThelP program, $^{34}$ the zero-point vibrational energy were scaled with factor of 0.967 reported by Alecu et al. $^{35}$ for the M06-2X/6-31+G(d,p) level of theory, which is most similar to the level of theory employed here. Due to inaccuracies in frequency calculations, $E_{\mathrm{A}}$ which is not sensitive to these inaccuracies, is more suitable in our comparison of reaction mechanisms than frequency factor. Differences in reaction rate coefficients are primarily due to differences in $E_{\mathrm{A}}$ . Thus, we focused on comparison of $E_{\mathrm{A}}$ in this work.

3.2. Initial Reaction Pathways. The initial elementary reaction pathway of glucose pyrolysis is shown in Figure 2. During $\beta$ -D-glucose pyrolysis, the most favored first-step reaction mechanism is the cleavage of the C1-O ring bond. Then, D-glucose (P-A) goes through 1,2-dehydration, pinacol rearrangement dehydration,[36] and isomerization[37] to form $C_6$ precursors, which lead to the formation of the two furans via subsequent reactions. As shown in Figure 2, isomerization of D-glucose into D-fructose (P-B), 3-ketohexose (P-C), and 4-ketohexose (P-D) are favored, and dehydration of D-glucose into P-F and P-E are likely to occur. Meanwhile, the formation of P-N1 and P-N2 are less competitive for their high energy barriers over $320~\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ .

As the key $C_4$ products, the formation of HBL and 3FO are related to some $C_2$ products such as hydroxyacetaldehyde (HAA), 1,2-ethenediol (ED), and ketene which release $C_4$ fragments during the reaction process. Thus, the subsequent pathways were classified into two mechanisms: the $C_4$ intermediate mechanism (Path-A to Path-F) and the $C_6$ -cyclization mechanism (Path-G to Path-I), as shown in Figure 3.

In the $C_4$ intermediate mechanism, the $C_6$ precursors (P-A to P-F) form $C_4$ intermediates through C-C bond scission. In general, during glucose pyrolysis, C-C bond scission might occur via direct scission, retro-Diels-Alder, retro-aldol reaction, or 1,3-dehydration. Based on previous computational studies, 1,3-dehydration, retro-Diels-Alder, and retro-aldol reaction are supposed to be more likely to occur in the pyrolysis of glucose.[19-21] Thus, these mechanisms were considered in subsequent pathways. Path-E and Path-F describe the formation pathways of $C_4$ fragments that competes with the formation of fructose and involves two dehydration reactions of glucose at the second step. A-1-i1 then goes the same subsequent reactions as Path-A to form HBL and 3FO with overall energy barriers of 285.15 and $280.80\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ , respectively. The activation energy of the rate-determining step (TS-A1a1) for HBL is $250.60\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ , close to the enol-keto tautomerization in formation of 3-hydroxypropanone from glycerol.[36] P-F undergoes retro-aldol reaction to form F-i1 (A-1-i2a) and ED with the scission of the C2-C3 bond. And F-i1 subsequently undergoes hydrogenation-cyclization to form HBL with overall energy barrier of $300.04\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ . The

rate-determining step for this pathway is pinacol rearrangement dehydration (TS-2) with the activation energy of 280.22 $\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ which is close to that $(272.80\mathrm{kJ / mol})$ in the study of Nimlos et al. $^{36}$

3.3.2. $C_6$ -Cyclization Mechanism. The cyclization first mechanism are investigated for the formation of HBL and 3FO in Path-G, Path-H, and Path-I. As shown in Figure 9A, P-A can go through cyclization (TS-G1) reaction with 4-H (OH) shift to 1-O and form G-i1 or H-i1. G-i1 will then decompose into 3FO via 1,3-dehydration (TS-G2) between 3-OH and 5-H(OH) and subsequent pinacol rearrangement dehydration with overall barrier of $290.12 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{mol}$ . H-i1 can first undergo 1,2-dehydration (TS-H2) between 3-OH and 2-H to form H-i2, then the subsequent Grob fragmentation (TS-H3) of H-i2 produces H-i3 and leads to the formation of 3FO via TS-H4. The rate-determining step of Path-H is TS-H2 with an overall energy barrier of $309.97 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{mol}$ , which indicates that the reaction is less kinetically favored than Path-G. The formation pathway (Path-I) of HBL from the cyclization first mechanism is shown in Figure 9B; after the initial cyclization between 3-O and 6-H(OH), the generated intermediate I-i1 decomposes into I-i2 and HAA through 1,3-dehydration between 2-H(OH) and 4-OH, I-i2 will then undergo keto-enol tautomerization to form HBL. Compared with Path-G and Path-H, P-I is less kinetically favored for the overall energy barrier of $326.30 \mathrm{~kJ} / \mathrm{mol}$ .

For HBL, the subsequent reactions of Path-E and Path-A1 showed that HBL is less competitive than 3FO. Meanwhile, Path-F can form HBL with high selectivity. Third, in Path-E, the intermediate P-E can also be easily transformed to 3-deoxy-glucosone via isomerization, which is the vital intermediate for the formation of FF and HMF in glucose pyrolysis.[12,43] Thus, Path-A2-C is more competitive than Path-E-B at $500^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , and Path-F is more competitive than Path-E-A and Path-A1, which means that HBL and 3FO mainly come from C3-C6 of glucose. # 4. CONCLUSIONS

In the present study, the possible formation pathways involving different intermediates, carbon sources of 3-hydroxy- $\gamma$ - butyrolactone and $3-(2H)$ -furanone are investigated by DFT

method at the $\mathrm{M}062x / 6 - 311++\mathrm{g}(\mathrm{d},\mathrm{p})$ level and pyrolysis of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ -labeled glucose. The following conclusions can be obtained.

(1) PY-GC×GC-TOF/MS pyrolysis of D-glucose indicates that the yields of HBL and 3FO are not negligible, they can be obtained from similar pathways, and they are competitive with HAA.   
(2) P-F is the intermediate of HBL, which undergoes retro-aldol reaction to form F-i1. Then F-i1 subsequently goes through hydrogenation-cyclization to form 3FO with $E_{\mathrm{A}}$ of $283.88~\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ at $500^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ .   
(3) P-A (D-glucose) and A-2-i2 (erythrose) are the intermediates of 3FO. In Path-A2-C, P-A goes through a retro-aldol reaction to obtain the $C_4$ fragment A-2-i1 from C3-C6 and ED with 3-H(OH) shifts to the carbonyl group and the scission of the C2-C3 bond. A-2-i1 then is preferred to undergo ring formation reaction and subsequent dehydration to form 3FO. The $E_{\mathrm{A}}$ of this pathway is $283.88~\mathrm{kJ / mol}$ at $500^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ .   
(4) DFT calculation indicates that the formation of HBL and that of 3FO are competitive in glucose pyrolysis. And, HBL and 3FO are mainly derived from C3-C6 of glucose in pyrolysis at typical pyrolysis temperatures.
