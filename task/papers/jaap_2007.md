# Carbohydrate pyrolysis mechanisms from isotopic labeling Part 1: The pyrolysis of glycerin: Discovery of competing fragmentation mechanisms affording acetaldehyde and formaldehyde and the implications for carbohydrate pyrolysis

John B. Paine III*, Yezdi B. Pithawalla, John D. Naworal, Charles E. Thomas Jr.1

Philip Morris USA, Research Center, P.O. Box 26583, Richmond, VA 23261, USA

Received 11 January 2007; accepted 29 March 2007

Available online 6 April 2007

# Abstract

The flash pyrolysis of glycerin was investigated by the use of isotopic labeling with $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ , in conjunction with GC/MS analysis of the products. The formation of acetaldehyde and acrolein was shown to occur by unimolecular reaction. Acetaldehyde was found to be produced by at least two competing mechanisms. Mechanism ‘‘A’’ delivered C-2 of glycerin to the carbonyl group of acetaldehyde, whereas mechanism ‘‘B’’ delivered C-2 of glycerin to the methyl group of acetaldehyde. Formaldehyde was exclusively derived from C-1 or C-3 by either mechanism. The partition between the two mechanistic paths was found to be influenced by the presence of potassium salts and acids, but not by the presence of benzoyl peroxide or galvanoxyl. Mechanism ‘‘A’’ is postulated to be a concerted cyclic version of the Grob Fragmentation, proceeding through an intermediate which is hydrogen-bonded between the 1- and 3-hydroxyl groups to simultaneously generate enol-acetaldehyde, formaldehyde and $_ \mathrm { H _ { 2 } O }$ . Mechanism ‘‘B’’ as favored by the presence of alkali is postulated to involve hydrogen-bonding between adjacent hydroxyl groups and to be an alkaline version of the pinacol rearrangement followed by retro-aldol fragmentation of the intermediary 3-oxopropoxide anion. These mechanistic classes are both fundamentally important, not only for their effect on glycerin, but for being able to provide numerous means of initial pyrolytic carbon–carbon bond breakage along carbohydrate carbon-chains, given the numerous 1,2,3-triol interactions that are possible. Further nomenclature is introduced to refine the distinctions among isotopomers and isotopologs (ipsomer, ipsolog, naturalomer, ‘‘nominal’’ isotopolog, ubiquilog), as extensions of a concept previously adopted by IUPAC.

# 1. Introduction

Carbohydrate pyrolysis chemistry occurs widely wherever biomass is combusted [1,2] or food is cooked [3]. Such chemistry is also relevant to the formation of cigarette smoke [4]. The mechanisms involved in this chemistry are therefore of considerable importance to understand. However, despite decades of research, understanding of the fundamental underlying reaction mechanisms is still rudimentary. This paper focuses on the pyrolysis of glycerin (1), which possesses much

of the functionality of a carbohydrate. The compact nature of glycerin relative to carbohydrates such as D-glucose (2) (Fig. 2) limits the degrees of freedom available to it, and makes its behavior more readily understood. With glycerin lacking the carbonyl functionality of D-glucose, the pyrolysis chemistry of glycerin is especially relevant to the chemistry occurring at the reduced end of the glucose molecule. As we will demonstrate below, pyrolytic carbon–carbon bond breakage in glycerin to form acetaldehyde unexpectedly occurs by at least two competing mechanisms, sufficiently similar in kinetics so as to provide two significant channels of reaction. These mechanisms are able to provide numerous differing modes of initial carbon–carbon bond-breakage within carbohydrates, and the consequences of this will be reported in the three following papers on the pyrolysis of D-glucose [5–7].

Here, we report the results of the flash pyrolysis of glycerin isotopologs [8–10], variously labeled with $^ { 1 3 } \dot { \mathrm { C } }$ . GC/MS was used to identify the products and determine the extent of label incorporation into the products, and the location of label therein.

Glycerin pyrolysis (without isotopic labeling) has been studied in the past, most notably by Stein et al. [11]. These investigators pyrolyzed glycerin in the gas-phase (with steam, in the absence of oxygen) at various temperatures and determined a mass balance for their results. The principal products at $6 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ were acrolein (3) $( 5 2 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ and acetaldehyde (4) $( 4 8 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ . Formaldehyde (5) was detected but could not be quantified in the experiment. At $6 7 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , the acrolein $( 3 9 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ and acetaldehyde $( 3 8 \mathrm { m o l \% } )$ were accompanied by further decomposition products, carbon monoxide $( 3 5 \mathrm { m o l \% } )$ , hydrogen $( 2 9 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ , ethylene $( 1 0 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ and methane $( 5 \mathrm { m o l } \% )$ ). Separate experiments showed that under their pyrolysis conditions, acrolein fragmented to ethylene and carbon monoxide, and acetaldehyde fragmented to methane and carbon monoxide. Free-radical mechanisms were invoked to explain both fragmentations. Left unstated was the presumption that formaldehyde underwent similar fragmentation to form hydrogen and carbon monoxide. The use of nitric oxide as a free-radical scavenger was explored to try to differentiate which products might arise as a result of freeradical processes. That study found that nitric oxide influenced the rate of formation of acrolein, but not that of acetaldehyde. Despite the lack of influence of nitric oxide on acetaldehyde formation, Stein et al. [11] postulated a radical bond cleavage mechanism to explain even the formation of acetaldehyde. Their one mechanism placed C-2 of glycerin into the aldehyde group of acetaldehyde. The rate of disappearance of glycerin was consistent with a first order mechanism. The reported activation energy $( 5 5 . 6 \pm 5 . 5 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l } )$ ) was not consistent with the energy needed to homolytically cleave the glycerin C-1/C-2 bond.

Furthermore, the above work did not establish the extent to which carbon–carbon bond connectivity might have been maintained during the pyrolytic process; such was merely presumed. However, the observed Arrhenius constant $( 1 . 5 2 \pm 0 . 1 5 \times 1 0 ^ { 1 3 } )$ was deemed consistent with a unimolecular reaction. The availability of $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 3 }$ -glycerin allows unimolecularity to be established directly, as will be demonstrated in this work.

# 1.1. Introduction of new nomenclature to describe isotopically-substituted substances

To ease further discussion in the use of isotopic labeling, we need to introduce and define nomenclature that will be used in this series of papers [5–7]. Previously, we introduced a new nomenclatural distinction to be made in the formerly broad concept of ‘‘isotopomers’’ [8,9]. ‘‘Isotopomers’’ as then defined encompassed relationships between two or more substances that were of several distinguishable kinds. Some isotopomers have identical overall isotopic composition, and thus have identical exact-mass molecular weights. Other isotopomers

could possess different overall isotopic compositions for each member of the set, and therefore have non-identical exact-mass molecular weights.

This latter group seemed to consist more of ‘‘isotopic homologs’’ rather than ‘‘isotopic isomers’’, and so the term ‘‘isotopolog’’ was coined to describe these, and their mutual relationship. True ‘‘isotopic isomers’’ (the isotopomers which have identical overall isotopic composition) were relegated to a redefined concept of ‘‘isotopomer’’. These distinctions were adapted by IUPAC [10]. (IUPAC further noted that ‘‘isotopomers’’ as redefined could be further distinguished as ‘‘regio’’, ‘‘stereo’’, ‘‘enantio’’, or ‘‘diastereo’’.)

‘‘Isotopomer’’ and ‘‘isotopolog’’ are both relative terms, describing the relationships of two or more substances. A simple example of two (regio) isotopomers as now defined would be the two isomers of ethane- $\cdot d _ { 2 }$ : ${ \mathrm { C H } } _ { 3 } { \mathrm { C H D } } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { D C H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { C H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { D }$ (where C and H are $^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C }$ and $\mathrm { ^ { 1 } H }$ specifically). Isotopologs are exemplified by the relationship of $\mathrm { C H } _ { 4 }$ , $\mathrm { C H } _ { 3 } \mathrm { D }$ , or $\mathrm { C H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { D } _ { 2 }$ , etc. to each other. ‘‘Isotopomer’’ and ‘‘isotopolog’’ may be used to describe individual molecules or molecular fragments, or populations of any of these.

In analogy to the ‘‘identity function’’ in mathematics, it is thought that the ‘‘point of reference’’ in any discussion of isomers or homologs should be given nomenclature to ease a discussion of these. The proposed terms are ‘‘ipsomer’’ and ‘‘ipsolog’’, respectively, from the Latin ‘‘ipso’’, meaning ‘‘self’’. These terms were devised for use with isotopomers and isotopologs, respectively, but could be used more broadly for discussions of isomers and homologs of all kinds. Thus, an ‘‘ipsomer’’ or ‘‘ispsolog’’ is the isomer or homolog, respectively, that is the point of reference for a topic under discussion. ‘‘Ipsomer’’ and ‘‘ipsolog’’ are also amenable for use to describe individual molecules, molecular fragments, or populations of these.

Mass spectral studies currently typically rely on the use of reference materials that are ‘‘unlabeled’’, but which in practice contain isotopic content that is of natural abundance ratio. However, as technology progresses, reference materials are coming available that may consist of high-purity dominant isotopes such as $\mathrm { ^ { 1 } H }$ , $^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C }$ , $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { \dot { N } }$ or $^ { 1 6 } \mathrm { O }$ . To allow facile distinction between such high-purity isotopologs and the ‘‘natural abundance’’ isotopologs, it is proposed to name the latter ‘‘naturalomers’’. (‘‘Naturalomer’’ is more melliphonous than ‘‘naturalolog’’, which might otherwise be the better choice of term for mnemonic reasons.) The term ‘‘naturalomer’’ specifically refers to populations of molecules or molecular fragments. The word is intended to be used interchangeably as such, both as a noun and as an adjective, e.g. ‘‘glycerin naturalomer’’ and ‘‘naturalomer glycerin’’ will be used interchangeably and have identical meaning.

In practice, the same natural abundance ‘‘isotopic impurities’’ that are present in the naturalomer are typically present in the ‘‘unlabeled’’ positions of commercial samples of isotopologs. It is proposed to denote such isotopologs as ‘‘nominal’’ isotopologs. ‘‘Nominal’’ isotopologs specifically refer to populations of isotopologs, rather than individual molecules. The above-defined terms will be used throughout this series of papers.

Further nomenclatural distinctions are possible, but will not be used here. For example, that isotopolog consisting solely of the most abundant isotope of each of its constituent elements, might be the ‘‘megalolog’’, from the Greek ‘‘megalos’’ for ‘‘enormous’’. A ‘‘megalolog’’ could be either an individual molecule or molecular fragment, or a population thereof. Given that definition, the ‘‘naturalomer’’ would be the ‘‘nominal’’ megalolog (a population), particularly for species comprised solely of C, H, N and/or O. The most abundant isomer or homolog in any discussion of a mixed population of these could be, respectively, denoted the ‘‘maximer’’ (or perhaps ‘‘megalomer’’) or the ‘‘maxilog’’. All of the above definitions are summarized in Table 1.

‘‘Uniformly-labeled’’ isotopologs might also benefit from additional nomenclature. Uniform labeling of an absolute (ca. $100 \%$ ) nature is most commonly associated with $^ 2 \mathrm { D }$ or $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ . It is proposed to denominate ‘‘uniformly labeled’’ isotopologs as ‘‘ubiquilogs’’, and preface these with the name of the isotopic element involved in the labeling, e.g. ‘‘deuterio-ubiquilog’’ or ‘‘13carbo-ubiquilog’’. By contrast, isotopic labeling with $\bar { ^ 3 } \mathrm { H }$ or $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ typically involves significant dilution with non-radioactive isotopes even in the ‘‘labeled’’ positions, to keep specific activities, stabilities, and economics manageable. The precise distribution of the isotopic label will reflect the stage at which the isotopic dilution process occurred, but mixtures of partiallylabeled isotopologs will be a common result, and such mixtures can be considered to be ‘‘nominally-uniformly’’ labeled, albeit in several possible senses, perhaps needing further nomenclatural refinement. Nomenclaturally, without such further

refinements, such materials could be referred to as a ‘‘nominal tritio-ubiquilog’’ or ‘‘nominal 14carbo-ubiquilog’’.

In addition, where needed for emphasis we adopt the practice of indicating the purity of an isotope, if known, as a superscript preceding the mass weight of the isotope in question. Thus, natural abundance carbon in a naturalomer might be denoted in a chemical formula as $\begin{array}{c} 9 8 . 9 \% 1 2 _ { \bigcirc }  \\ { ~ } \end{array}$ . A high purity uniformly labeled nsubstrate (13carbo-ubiquilog) might be indicated as $^ { 9 9 . 9 + \% 1 3 } { \mathrm { C } } _ { n }$ . Using this convention, the formula for a nominal isotopolog might be of the form $^ { 9 9 . 9 + \% 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 } ^ { 9 8 . 9 \% 1 2 } \mathrm { C } _ { n - 1 }$ 98.9% 12C .

# 3. Calculation of isotopic content of products

The choice of mathematical algorithm used to determine the extent of isotopic incorporation and isotopic location within the product molecules was influenced by the presence of prominent $\mathbf M - 1$ fragment ion peaks found for aldehydes. The mathematics involve comparison of corresponding individual peaks within normalized parent peak patterns for analyte and reference. These calculations were performed using EXCEL $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ spreadsheets, starting with the ion-current intensities tabulated for each mass-spectrum, spanning the range from 12 D to the far end of the molecular ion region.

The algorithm (Eq. (2)), which we trivially derived from first principles, is derived from the expectation that to a first approximation, an isotopolog (parent ion or molecularfragment) with a single added carbon isotopic label of unit higher mass should show a normalized mass spectral ionabundance pattern in the ion-region under consideration identical to that of the ipsomer, but with the said pattern displaced to higher mass by 1 Da. (One of the authors has used this treatment in the past to compute the evolution of mass spectral patterns over time as a porphyrin underwent kinetic deuteriation at the 5, 10, 15 and 20 ‘‘meso’’ CH positions [14]) For fragment ions, it is assumed that there is no dilution with fragments unrelated to the ones under scrutiny; the extent of validity of this assumption for any particular region of the mass spectrum will be highly idiosyncratic to a particular substrate. It is also assumed that the ipsolog and its isotopolog will not be subject to an isotope effect with respect to the formation of total ion current within the parent ion

envelope in the mass spectrometer, such that there will be identical response factors for both. It is further assumed that there will be no isotope effect governing the loss of individual protons in the parent-ion (or other) region(s), as a result of the presence of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ . We have found that this last assumption introduces a slight error into the results, as can be observed from an inspection of the data in Table 2, where the parent ion intensity ratios are compared for the acetaldehyde, formaldehyde and acrolein naturalomers and 13carbo-ubiquilogs. It will be seen that, with the exception of formaldehyde, the presence of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ enhances the relative formation of $\mathbf M - 1$ peaks, which leads to the algorithm slightly underestimating the content of label. (We have observed for acetonitrile, of which a complete set of possible nominal $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ isotopologs is available commercially, that $\mathbf M - 1$ is enhanced whenever a $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C H } _ { 3 }$ -group is present, but is unaffected by the presence of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C N }$ functionality. Thus the enhancement appears to require direct bonding of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { \bar { C } }$ to the proton being lost in order to have a significant effect. In the case of acetonitrile, a similar enhancement of M-2 and M-3 fragments was also observed [15]. We suspect that this is an important manifestation of the ‘‘magnetic isotope effect’’ [16] brought on by the magnetic moment of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } .$ .)

Finally, the algorithm neglects slight differences in relative $\mathbf M + 1$ peak intensity between ipsolog (naturalomer) and (nominal) isotopolog, the latter having one less position available for natural-abundance $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ than the former. Similar considerations exist for the $\mathbf { M } + 2$ and higher peaks, but here are insignificant.

The relevant ion-cluster pattern (ideally bounded by ionmasses of zero intensity) for ipsolog (usually the naturalomer) and analyte is each locally normalized; e.g. all the relative peak intensities (ion-currents) $\pmb { a } _ { i }$ within the pattern of $\pmb { n }$ consecutive peaks of ascending mass of the reference ipsolog are such that: $\textstyle \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { n } a _ { i } = 1$ . In a context of application to GC/MS, it is technically superior to normalize locally with respect to a single ion cluster, rather than globally, for the spectrum as a whole. The ion cluster is far more likely to be ‘‘pure’’ [e.g. free from interference by co-eluting species], than is the spectrum as a whole. Also, in the context of GC/MS, there is less likely to be concentration variation during the course of the spectral scan, over the relatively short scan range encompassed in the molecular ion region envelope, or any other local ion-cluster. In an assumed binary population of molecular species wherein the mole-fraction of the singly-labeled isotopolog is $\boldsymbol { x }$ , and the mole-fraction of the unlabeled ipsolog is ${ \bf 1 } - x$ , then the observed normalized intensity $( I _ { m } )$ of any given peak of mass $\pmb { m }$ of the analyte is given by Eq. (1).

$$
\boldsymbol {I} _ {m} = \boldsymbol {a} _ {m} (1 - \boldsymbol {x}) + \boldsymbol {a} _ {m - 1} (\boldsymbol {x}). \tag {1}
$$

It is trivial to prove that with $\boldsymbol { x }$ and $\pmb { a } _ { i }$ so defined, the complete population of $I _ { m }$ must sum to unity, and therefore be normalized a priori. Thus, by using the observed normalized values of $I _ { m }$ for the analyte, it is mathematically valid to solve for $\boldsymbol { x }$ in accordance with Eq. (2), which is readily evaluated in an EXCEL $\textsuperscript { \textregistered }$ spreadsheet for every peak of interest in the mass spectrum.

$$
x = \frac {I _ {m} - a _ {m}}{a _ {m - 1} - a _ {m}} \tag {2}
$$

The values for $\boldsymbol { x }$ are meaningful to the extent that the underlying assumptions are obeyed. For the largest peaks in the parent-ion cluster, it is usual to find excellent agreement for the values of $\boldsymbol { x }$ . This is particularly true for ion-currents at high ratios of signal to noise. Background subtraction algorithms applied during the collection of the original data, and some errors resulting from the assumptions may make the calculations less reliable for the smaller peaks. Thus, calculations involving the smaller peaks in the cluster are typically less in agreement with $\boldsymbol { x }$ as determined from the largest peaks, and are discounted. In some cases, particularly for species with $\mathbf M - 1$ ion intensity comparable to that of M, the algorithm may ‘‘blow up’’ specifically for the M peak, as a result of the magnetic isotope effects governing the relative abundance of the $\mathbf M - 1$ peaks. [However, in this circumstance, the values for $\boldsymbol { x }$ as determined at $\mathbf M - 1$ and $\mathbf { M } + 1$ will tend to agree closely.]

Although the algorithm can provide a number for every peak in the mass spectrum, only a limited subset of this data is meaningful, generally that arising from strong mass fragments with little interference from neighboring masses.

For the simple analytes described in this work, dilution of fragment ions by irrelevant alternate fragmentation modes was not an issue. However, for the more complex products arising from glucose pyrolysis, this problem arises, and will be discussed further in the papers which follow [5–7].

# 4. Results and discussion

# 4.1. Results from the electrically heated AccordTM prototype cigarette smoking system

Experiment 1, our first experiment in the use of $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ to follow pyrolytic mechanism, was run to ascertain the extent to which glycerin pyrolysis contributed to the acrolein content of the cigarette smoke produced under the smoking conditions intrinsic to the AccordTM electrically heated cigarette prototype system. To maximize the visibility of carbon-13 label in this system, $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 3 }$ -glycerin was chosen as the labeled substrate. Depending upon the original glycerin content of the cigarette, the two models tested produced acrolein that was either about $20 \%$ or about $40 \%$ derived from the glycerin. Significantly, the acrolein produced by these two models bore either three labels, or none. There was essentially no isotopic mixing between the acrolein derived from the glycerin, and that derived from the unlabeled remainder of the cigarette. Thus, these two sources of acrolein were forming acrolein by independent means. Also observed among the products was doubly labeled acetaldehyde and singly labeled formaldehyde, admixed with the unlabeled versions of both molecules. These observations carried an implication that the chemistry involved would be unimolecular. Direct proof of this came from the results of Experiment 2.

# 4.2. Experiment 2: establishing the maintenance of carbon–carbon bond connectivity during pyrolytic processes

The isotopic scrambling experiment consisted of the copyrolysis of a homogeneous blend of naturalomer (98.9% 12C ) $( ^ { 9 8 . 9 \% 1 2 } \mathrm { C } _ { 3 } )$ and 13carbo-ubiquilog $\binom { 9 9 + 9 6 } { 2 } \times ( C _ { 3 } )$ glycerin. This experiment was intended to establish the extent of unimolecularity (with respect to carbon) for the formation mechanisms of the various products. Isotopic cross-products, which would be evidence for the intervention of multi-molecular processes, were found to be minimal for acetaldehyde (4) and acrolein (3). The parent ion region of acrolein naturalomer consists of two major peaks of about equal intensity at m/z 56 and $5 5 ~ \mathrm { D }$ , with zero intensity at $m / z 5 4 \mathrm { ~ D ~ }$ . For $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 3 }$ -acrolein, the corresponding two ions occur at m/z 59 and 58 D. Singly labeled acrolein (m/z 57 and 56 D) and doubly labeled acrolein $( m / z 5 8$ and 57 D) would each have a peak at 57 D. The essentially complete absence of an $m / z 5 7 \mathrm { D }$ peak in the observed spectrum attests to the negligible isotopic scrambling in this system, by inspection. Furthermore, the essentially complete absence of an m/z 57 D ion fragment for

acrolein derived from the pyrolysis of $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 3 }$ -glycerin alone attests to the near $100 \%$ isotopic purity for the contained $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ . The mass spectrum of the acetaldehyde formed from the isotopic scrambling experiment presented the parent ion region (consisting of M and $\mathbf M - 1$ ions of comparable intensity) for $^ { 1 2 } { \bf C } _ { 2 }$ and $\bar { 1 3 } _ { \mathbf { C } _ { 2 } }$ -acetaldehyde as a phalanx of four adjacent peaks spanning the range of $m / z = 4 3  – 4 6 \mathrm { ~ I ~ }$ Da. This needed mathematical deconvolution into ${ } ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C } _ { 2 }$ , ${ } ^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 } { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 }$ and ${ } ^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 2 }$ components to show the near absence of a $^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 }$ species, beyond that which would result from the natural abundance $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ in glycerin naturalomer.

Isotopic scrambling of carbon was also minimal for the other pyrolysis products containing two or three carbons. Unimolecularity with respect to carbon carries an implication that carbon–carbon bond connectivity was maintained during a mechanistic process. (Exceptions to this would involve the intervention of unimolecular rearrangement mechanisms.) In the absence of evidence to the contrary, unscrambled products are deemed to have maintained their carbon–carbon bond connectivity during the course of the pyrolysis. However, no experiments were performed to develop information as to the unimolecularity with respect to the hydrogen or oxygen atom content of the system.

A particularly important conclusion is that observation of a lack of isotopic scrambling in this experiment rules out any importance for mechanistic processes involving carbon–carbon bond formation from a pair of independent free radicals for the products in question. This agrees with the nitric oxide evidence presented earlier [11] with respect to acetaldehyde formation. Furthermore, there was no discernable increase in isotopic scrambling found when the experiment was repeated in the presence of free radical initiators such as benzoyl peroxide or free radical traps such as galvanoxyl.

# 4.5. Change of mechanistic preference over time; effect of additives

When originally examined, the pyrolysis experiments preferentially delivered C-2 of glycerin to the aldehyde group of acetaldehyde, and C-1 or C-3 to the methyl group (Table 4, ‘‘original results’’), in accordance with mechanism ‘‘A’’. $2 \mathrm { - } ^ { 1 3 } \bar { \mathrm { C } } _ { 1 }$ -glycerin produced $1 - { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 ^ { - } }$ and 2- $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 1 }$ -acetaldehyde in about a 72–28 ratio, whereas $^ { 1 , 3 - ^ { 1 3 } } { \mathrm { C } } _ { 2 }$ -glycerin produced $1 - { } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 { } ^ { - } }$ and 2- $^ { 1 3 } { \bf C } _ { 1 }$ -acetaldehyde in about a 48–52 ratio. The considerable divergence in the proportion between the two competing pathways to acetaldehyde between 2- ${ } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } _ { 1 }$ -glycerin and 1,3- $\bar { } ^ { 1 3 } \bar { \mathrm { C } } _ { 2 }$ -glycerin led to the notion that a significant isotope effect might be affecting the relative mechanistic partition. Two years later, when replicated with the same samples of glycerin which had been stored in glass in the intervening time (in a refrigerator, in the dark), mechanism ‘‘B’’ was found to have become dominant. The ratio had converged to symmetrical reciprocal equivalence for the two isotopologs, respectively, about 35–65 or about 65–35 for the two products in the two systems (Table 4, ‘‘replication columns’’). Thus, whatever isotope effect may have been embedded in the original data was clearly overwhelmed by ‘‘ageing’’ effects.

The change over time could potentially be ascribed to either of two possibilities. One of these could be the development of ‘‘peroxides’’ on standing in air over time. To test this hypothesis, the effect of a free radical initiator such as benzoyl peroxide, or of a free radical trap such as galvanoxyl on the pyrolysis was examined. This experiment rested on the assumption that these two additives might survive the pyrolysis conditions just long enough to have a visible influence, or at least be of comparable survivability to the hypothesized peroxides. Little significant effect was found. Table 5 presents the averaged data that was obtained for the same two aged glycerin isotopolog samples when examined in triplicate with either benzoyl peroxide or galvanoxyl. These results can also be seen to be fairly similar to those obtained in triplicate from the corresponding isotopologs of glycerin without additives, as shown in Table 4. The conclusion is that free radicals were not significantly involved in the partition between the two competing mechanisms.

The second hypothesis involved the notion that alkali metal cations such as sodium or potassium might be leaching from the glass into the glycerin upon storage over time. Such alkali content could be highly variable even in newly obtained samples, since the synthetic details, and the time elapsed since synthesis or bottling would not generally be known. The mechanism that was favored previously by the aging of the samples was also favored by the addition of potassium salts, with the bicarbonate having the most influence in this direction. Fresh distillation of the samples worked in the opposite direction, favoring the originally dominant mechanism (discussed below as ‘‘Mechanism A’’), but not entirely to the maximum extent previously observed.

None of the glycerin samples were analyzed for alkali. To be relevant, this would have to have been done contemporaneously with the pyrolyses, and at the time the work was done, this was an unanticipated need.

# 4.6. Mechanism ‘‘A’’, the Cyclic Grob Fragmentation of glycerin (1)

1,2,3-Triol systems such as glycerin are capable of intramolecular hydrogen-bonding between the hydroxyl groups. One of the hydrogen-bonding conformations occurs between the terminal (1 and 3) hydroxyl groups so as to generate a six-membered ring bonding arrangement. By forcing electron migration around this ring system, glycerin can be made to fragment in concerted electrocyclic fashion to generate formaldehyde (5), enolacetaldehyde (ethenol) (6), and water. Upon cooling and condensation, enol-acetaldehyde 6 reverts to the carbonyl tautomer 4, which is the observed product under our conditions (Scheme 2). By this unimolecular mechanism, C-2 of glycerin specifically ends up as the aldehyde carbon of acetaldehyde (4). The Cyclic Grob Fragmentation would be expected to be available for operation in either the gas phase or a condensed phase. Ethenol has only recently been proven to occur significantly in combustion flames [17].

Mechanism ‘‘A’’ bears some resemblance to the ‘‘Grob Fragmentation’’ [18,19]. The internal hydrogen bonding undoubtedly functions to improve the leaving abilities of the hydroxide anion, otherwise a poor leaving group, not usually associated with the Grob reaction. We therefore chose to name this electrocyclic fragmentation the ‘‘Cyclic Grob Fragmentation’’ and will refer to it as such for the remainder of this series of papers. Mark Nimlos et al. performed calculations as to the energy barriers associated with this mechanism, and their results have recently been published elsewhere [20]. In its neutral (unprotonated) version, the fragmentation is endothermic $\Delta _ { \mathrm { r e a c t } } E _ { 0 \mathrm { K } } = 2 5 . 4 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l } )$ with an energy barrier $\Delta _ { \mathrm { T S } } E _ { 0 \mathrm { K } } = 6 5 . 2 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ . The protonated (cationic) version, leading to protonated formaldehyde, is endothermic by $2 7 . 7 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ with an energy barrier of $3 6 . 2 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ [20].

The inherent symmetry of glycerin masks the asymmetry of the mechanism caused by the hydrogen-bonding arrangement. There are two possible orientations of the 1,3-hydrogen

bonding within a general 1,2,3-triol system, leading to carbon– carbon bond breakage in two possible manners within any such functional moiety. The consequences of this asymmetry when the Cyclic Grob Fragmentation is extended to unsymmetrical 1,2,3-triol systems widely found among carbohydrates will be shown in the following two papers to rationalize the formation of $\mathrm { C } _ { 1 } { - } \mathrm { C } _ { 2 }$ and $\mathrm { C } _ { 3 } { \mathrm { - C } } _ { 4 }$ containing carbonyl-compounds, respectively, from the pyrolysis of D-glucose [5,6].

# 4.7. Mechanism ‘‘B’’: Possibility 1: the sequential fragmentation of 1,2,3-triols by tandem alkaline pinacolrearrangement and retro-aldol fragmentation (TAPRRAF) reaction

Mechanism ‘‘B’’, whereby C-2 of glycerin becomes the methyl-group of acetaldehyde, has at least two possible explanations which will be discussed in turn. (The determination of the relative importance of these will require the application of computational chemistry [20].) One of the possibilities that is consistent with the favorable influence of alkalis, starts with a second possible hydrogen-bonded conformation of glycerin, involving adjacent hydroxyl groups to provide a five-membered ring. The particularly relevant conformation has the terminal hydroxymethyl alcohol proton hydrogen-bonded to the oxygen atom of central hydroxy group. The mechanism postulates 1,2-hydride migration from the nonhydrogen bonded terminal hydroxymethyl group, present as an alkoxide anion, that nucleophilically displaces the central hydroxy-group, assisted by the hydrogen-bonding. This is depicted in Scheme 3. The resulting 3-oxopropoxide anion (7) is the familiar intermediate of the aldol reaction, and should be subject to thermal reversal, to afford acetaldehyde (enolate) and formaldehyde. This possibility can be considered as a sequential alkaline version of the pinacol rearrangement feeding into a retro-aldol fragmentation. The involvement of the relatively non-volatile alkali metal cation, and the need to solvate this to the substrate may relegate this mechanism to occurrence within a condensed phase, or superheated condensed or supercritical phase.

As for the ‘‘Cyclic Grob Fragmentation’’, mechanism ‘‘B’’ is also inherently asymmetrical and provides two means of fragmenting any general asymmetric 1,2,3-triol system. # 4.9. Concerted single-step fragmentation of glycerin versus previous hypotheses as to mechanism

The mechanism proposed by Stein et al. [11] invoked a free-radical homolytic cleavage of the C-1/C-2 bond of glycerin to provide a hydroxylmethylene radical (precursor to formaldehyde and a hydrogen radical), and a 1,2-dihydroxyethyl radical. The latter was deemed ready to lose hydroxy radical with the formation of enol-acetaldehyde. The enol would result in the formation of acetaldehyde whose carbonyl carbon was derived from the C-2 atom of glycerin. Glossed over in their analysis was the circumstance that the bond to be homolytically cleaved might have needed upwards of 90 kcal/mol to break, whereas their determined activation energy for the disappearance of glycerin was only about 55 kcal/mol. Their work with nitric oxide as radical scavenger ruled out substantial radical involvement in acetaldehyde formation.

Nimlos’s recent calculations have confirmed our surmise that concerted electrocyclic reactions are of primary importance in explaining glycerin fragmentation, to the exclusion of free-radical alternatives [20].

Some non-cyclic fragmentations related to the Grob fragmentation have been speculated on in the past [23,24] with respect to carbohydrate pyrolysis. These proposed reactions would not have such favorable energetics as the concerted cyclic version.

# 4.10. Other recent reports of glycerin pyrolysis

Glycerin was among the ingredients of tobacco products whose pyrolytic behaviors were evaluated recently by Baker and Bishop [25]. At their relatively slow heating rate of $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } / \mathrm { s }$ they reported almost exclusive distillation of glycerin. Carmines and Gaworski [26], using slightly more vigorous conditions, also reported mostly distillation, but with the formation of observable traces of acrolein or glycolaldehyde. Our conditions were more energetic, designed to provoke observable decomposition of glycerin within the 2-s timeconstraint of the pyrolysis, given the kinetics intrinsic to the reaction, which decomposition being expected to proceed by the most energetically favorable paths available. Given the high levels of glycerin often found in tobacco products [25,26], only a small absolute yield of decomposition products is needed to provide the levels observed. The levels of glycerin decomposition products we report for the AccordTM prototype are intrinsic to that product and the manner of its operation, and cannot be extrapolated directly to ‘‘lit-end’’ (e.g. conventional) cigarettes. Whether glycerin decomposition leads to observable products in a particular ‘‘lit-end’’ cigarette will only be settled by the use of isotopic labeling experiments, rather than by the use of statistical arguments, since there may be an offsetting decrease of acrolein or other product formation from the other carbohydrates in the presence of significant levels of glycerin. Even if glycerin itself manages to escape decomposition by distillation, its mechanisms of decomposition are still relevant to the behavior of carbohydrates whose lesser volatility forces them to be exposed to higher levels of heat.

For glycerin itself within a ‘‘lit-end’’ cigarette, the competition between decomposition versus intact distillation is a complex function of kinetic responses by both processes relative to a rapidly rising heat gradient imposed by the act of puffing. Under the static conditions inherent to ‘‘sidestream smoke’’ formation, the relative proportion of the two processes may change.

# 5. Conclusions

In conclusion, we have found by the use of isotopic labeling with $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ that the pyrolysis of glycerin leads to the unimolecular formation of acetaldehyde with both possible labeling patterns being produced simultaneously from any given symmetrical isotopolog. Depending upon the presence of additives, impurities, or catalysts, as many as four different mechanisms may be involved. Under our pyrolytic conditions, these mechanisms were competitive, and the relative mechanistic partition could be influenced in one direction by the addition of potassium salts, or of acidic phosphates or sulfates, and in the opposite direction by vacuum distillation of the substrate just prior to the pyrolysis, or by the influence of acidic sulfonates. Since the functionality of glycerin is also present in the reduced end of the higher carbohydrates, the principles involved in its fragmentation can be readily extrapolated to these. The papers which follow will show how initial carbon-chain breakage by these mechanisms provide easy rationalizations of the principal

labeling patterns that are observed to occur among the pyrolysis products [5–7]. These mechanisms are thus fundamental to an understanding of carbohydrate pyrolysis in general.

We have also used this paper as the opportunity to introduce new nomenclature. We believe that this nomenclature will be useful in the future to ease discussions in the area of isotopomers and isotopologs, and of isomers in general.
